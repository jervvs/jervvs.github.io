## The Governing Philosophy

Your four goals — body control, fitness, fat loss, and being pain-free — are not in conflict. They actually compound:

- **Fat loss** reduces the mass your body has to control, making movement easier
- **Fitness** (cardio base) makes every session recoverable and accelerates fat loss
- **Strength in full range** makes your mobility functional — you don't just get into positions, you own them
- **Movement practice** trains the nervous system's map of your body — and this is the piece most people never do

The key distinction: **body control is a neural skill, not a physical one.** A fatigued nervous system cannot learn movement. This is why this plan keeps strength work moderate (not maximal) and treats movement practice as sacred — never skipped, never rushed, never done when exhausted.

The measure of progress here is not weight lifted or reps hit. It's whether you move more freely, feel less pain, and inhabit your body more fully.

---

## The Three Pillars

### Pillar 1 — Movement Practice (Daily, 15–20 min)
This is the body control foundation. It runs every day because it has almost zero recovery cost and because neural adaptation requires frequent repetition. Think of it like language learning — you don't get fluent by cramming once a week.

### Pillar 2 — Pool + Zone 2 (Primary fat loss and fitness engine)
Swimming is uniquely valuable: it builds proprioception almost passively, forces you to feel your body moving through space, and burns calories with zero joint impact. Zone 2 on the bike builds your aerobic base and fat-burning machinery without stressing joints. These two sessions are the highest-leverage work in your week for weight loss and fitness.

### Pillar 3 — Bodyweight Strength in Full Range
Not for size. Not for performance metrics. To make your mobility functional — so the range you're building has strength inside it. A joint you can move but not control under load is an injury waiting to happen. Strength in full range closes that gap.

**Note on flexibility:** Life will shift days. That's fine. What matters is hitting weekly targets: 2 swim sprints, 1 Zone 2, 2 strength sessions, 1 calisthenics session, daily movement practice. The order above is optimised — but imperfect execution beats perfect planning.

**Why Friday for calisthenics:** It sits between Thursday's swim and Saturday's lower strength. The swim the day before doesn't heavily load the movements you'll do Friday, and Saturday's session is lower-body dominant — so there's no meaningful overlap. If you need to move it, Wednesday (replacing Zone 2) is the next best option, not Sunday before rest.

## Pillar 1 — Daily Movement Practice

Do this in the morning if possible, before the nervous system is loaded with other demands. If morning isn't realistic, any time works — just not immediately after heavy lifting.

This is not stretching. Every exercise is *active* — you are moving your own body into and through range, building neural ownership of those positions.

### The Daily 15–20 Min Flow

**1. Breathing Reset (2 min)**
Lie on your back, knees bent. Place one hand on chest, one on belly. Breathe so that your belly rises first, then ribs expand sideways (you should feel them press into the floor), then chest last. Exhale fully. This activates your diaphragm as a core stabiliser — the actual foundation of movement control that almost nobody trains.
- 10 slow breath cycles

**2. Spinal Segmentation (2 min)**
On hands and knees. Move through your spine one segment at a time — not just cat-cow as one wave, but trying to isolate each vertebra. This reconnects your nervous system to parts of your spine that most people have lost access to.
- 10 slow reps, extremely deliberate

**3. 90/90 Hip Rotations (3 min)**
Sit on the floor, both knees bent at 90 degrees (one in front, one to the side). Slowly rotate through your hips, switching sides. Feel where the resistance is — that's where you need to spend time.
- 5 transitions each direction, slow
- Hold any stuck point for 20–30s

**4. Deep Squat Exploration (3 min)**
Hold something fixed (door frame, rack) for balance if needed. Lower into a deep squat. Once there, explore: shift weight left and right, try to release your grip, tilt your pelvis forward and back, look around. The goal is to make the position feel *inhabited*, not survived.
- 3 min cumulative, broken into holds and movement

**5. Ground Transitions (3 min)**
Start standing. Lower yourself to the floor and get back up — but vary the path each time. Try: cross-legged sit to stand, side-lying roll to stand, squat to stand, kneeling to stand. Use your hands as little as possible.
This is the single best exercise for functional body control. It trains your whole system to coordinate getting in and out of positions — the kind of thing that predicts pain-free movement for decades.
- Aim for 10 transitions in 3 min, no rushing

**6. Shoulder Circles + Thoracic Rotation (2 min)**
Standing or sitting. Big, slow shoulder circles — full range, let the scapula move. Then thoracic rotation: sit cross-legged, hands behind head, rotate left and right from the mid-back (not the lower back).
- 10 circles each arm, 10 rotations each side

**7. One Deliberate Stretch (2 min)**
Pick one area that feels tight today. Hold it for 2 min with *active* engagement — meaning you're not just hanging into the stretch, you're gently contracting and releasing the muscle.
- Rotate through: hip flexors, hamstrings, lats, chest

### Extended Friday Session (25–30 min)
On Friday, repeat the full flow and then add:
- 5 min of slow, exploratory floor movement (rolling, crawling, reaching) — no goal, just moving
- 2 min passive hang from pull-up bar (spinal decompression)
- 3 min of any mobility area that's been limiting you this week

## Pillar 2 — Swim Sprint Sessions (Monday + Thursday)

Your current 800–1000m structure is already good. Don't inflate the volume. Quality is the entire point.

**Structure (both sessions):**
1. 200m easy warm-up (any stroke, focus on feeling the water)
2. 4×50m drill set — technique focus (catch, body rotation, kick timing)
3. Main set: 6–8×25m ALL OUT — full 2–3 min rest between each
4. Optional 2×50m easy cool-down

**Key principle:** When your sprint times drop noticeably (more than 2–3 seconds off your best), stop. Fatigued sprints don't build power — they build bad patterns. Quality over accumulation.

**Why swimming specifically helps body control:** Moving through water gives you constant proprioceptive feedback. You literally feel your body position through resistance. Over months, this builds an unusually refined sense of where your limbs are in space — which transfers to all other movement.

**Progress signal:** Faster 25m times, or a stroke that feels more connected and effortless, not more reps.

---

## Pillar 2 — Zone 2 (Wednesday)

**Protocol:** 40–45 min, bike preferred over running.

**Why bike over running:** At higher bodyweight, running Zone 2 puts significant compressive load on knees and ankles. The bike delivers the same aerobic adaptation with zero joint cost. Transition to running Zone 2 as you get lighter and your movement practice has built better joint stability.

**Intensity:** Conversational pace. Nose breathing throughout. If you're mouth-breathing, slow down — you've crossed into Zone 3, which doesn't build the fat-burning machinery you want.

**Progress signal:** Your heart rate at the same pace will gradually drop over weeks. That's the adaptation working — your cardiovascular system becoming more efficient.

## Pillar 3 — Strength Sessions

Two sessions per week. Strength work here has one job: make your mobility functional. Every exercise is chosen for its range of motion, not its muscle-building efficiency.

### Upper Strength + Core (Tuesday, ~55 min)

**Warm-up (5 min):** Dead hang 30s, 10 scapular retractions on the bar, 10 thoracic rotations

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Tempo | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|-------|
| Pull-Up (or assisted / negative) | 4×4–6 | 3 sec down | Full hang at bottom. Initiate with shoulder blades, not arms. |
| Inverted Row | 3×10 | 2 sec hold at top | Body straight. Feet on floor to start, elevated to progress. |
| Face Pull (band) | 3×15 | Controlled | Shoulder health. Elbows high. Never skip this. |
| Push-Up (incline to flat progression) | 3×8–10 | 3 sec down | Find the angle where 10 reps is honest work. |

**Core block (15 min, included in 55):**

| Exercise | Sets × Duration/Reps | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Dead Bug | 3×8/side | 2 sec pause each rep. Lower back glued to floor. |
| Hollow Body Hold | 3×20–40s | Knees tucked first, progress to legs extended. |
| Hanging Knee Raise | 3×8–10 | Initiate by curling pelvis under, not just lifting legs. |

### Lower Strength + Core (Saturday, ~55 min)

**Warm-up (5 min):** 90/90 hip rotations, deep squat hold 60s, hip flexor stretch 30s/side

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Tempo | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|-------|
| Hip Thrust | 4×8 | 2 sec squeeze at top | Primary glute builder. Drives fat loss and posterior chain control. |
| Romanian Deadlift | 4×6 | 3 sec down | Feel it in hamstrings. If lower back speaks first, reduce weight and focus on hinge. |
| Goblet Squat | 3×10 | 3 sec down, pause at bottom | Hold the bottom for 2 sec. This doubles as mobility work. |
| Single-Leg Glute Bridge | 3×10/side | Controlled | Corrects asymmetry. Harder than it looks. |

**Core block (15 min):**

| Exercise | Sets × Duration/Reps | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Dead Bug | 3×8/side | Same as Tuesday |
| Side Plank | 3×20–30s/side | Hip stacked. Progress to hip dips. |
| Glute Bridge Hold | 3×30s | Squeeze hard. Protects lower back. |

### Progression Rule
Each week, do ONE of the following per exercise — never all three:
- Add 1–2 reps
- Slow the eccentric by 1 second
- Add 2.5–5kg (loaded exercises only)

If you can't do any of those with clean form, repeat the same session. Form is the metric — not numbers.

## Friday — Calisthenics + Skills Session (~55 min)

### The Volume Principle
3 working sets per movement. Always stop 2–3 reps before failure. The moment your form shifts — hips dropping on push-ups, swinging on the bar, shoulders shrugging on dips — the set is over. Quality reps build the skill. Grinding reps build compensations.

Skills come first while the nervous system is completely fresh. Strength work follows. This is the reverse of what most people do, and it's the right order.

---

### Warm-Up (5 min)
- Dead hang: 2×20s (decompress, feel the shoulder pack)
- Scapular retractions on bar: 10 slow reps (arms straight, move only shoulder blades)
- 90/90 hip rotations: 5 each side
- 10 slow thoracic rotations (hands behind head, rotate from mid-back)

---

### Block 1 — Skill Practice (15–20 min)

Train 2–3 skills per session, rotating through them. Don't try to hit all five every Friday — depth beats breadth here. Each skill gets 4–5 focused attempts with full rest between.

**Rotation example:**
- Week 1: L-sit + Handstand
- Week 2: Skin the Cat + Elbow Lever
- Week 3: L-sit + Muscle-Up progression
- Week 4: Handstand + Skin the Cat

#### L-Sit
*What it builds: hip flexor strength, shoulder depression, core tension, body awareness*

| Stage | What it looks like | Hold target |
|-------|--------------------|-------------|
| 1 | Hands on floor beside hips, feet on floor, lift hips only | 3×10s |
| 2 | Hands on floor, one leg extended / one tucked | 3×10s |
| 3 | Both legs tucked, fully off floor | 3×15s |
| 4 | One leg extended, one tucked | 3×10s |
| 5 | Full L-sit, both legs extended | 3×10s+ |

**Key cue:** Push the floor away, shoulders down and back, ribs compressed. It's a pressing movement, not a hanging one.

**Unlock criterion to advance a stage:** 3 clean holds at target duration, no sagging, across 2 consecutive sessions.

#### Handstand
*What it builds: shoulder stability, proprioception, spatial awareness, core tension*

| Stage | What it looks like | Target |
|-------|--------------------|--------|
| 1 | Pike handstand (hips up, feet on floor, hands shoulder-width) | 3×20s, feel weight in hands |
| 2 | Wall handstand (back to wall, walk feet up) | 3×20–30s, body straight |
| 3 | Wall handstand (chest to wall, harder — builds real balance) | 3×20s |
| 4 | Kick-up attempts (spot yourself against wall) | 5 attempts, find the balance point |
| 5 | Freestanding holds (away from wall) | Build from 3s toward 30s |

**Key cue:** Spread your fingers wide, push through every knuckle. The handstand is controlled from the fingers, not the wrists.

**Most important thing:** Spend months at Stage 2 and 3. The instinct is to rush to freestanding. Don't. Wall handstand done with perfect body tension is building exactly the same neural patterns you'll need freestanding.

#### Skin the Cat
*What it builds: shoulder mobility through full rotation, scapular strength, body awareness in unfamiliar positions. This is one of the best movements for body control specifically.*

| Stage | What it looks like | Target |
|-------|--------------------|--------|
| 1 | Hang from bar, tuck knees, slowly rotate hips down and through (German hang tuck) | 3×3 slow rotations |
| 2 | Hold the German hang (knees still tucked) at bottom | 3×10s |
| 3 | Rotate through with straighter legs | 3×3 rotations |
| 4 | Full skin the cat (legs straight throughout) | 3×3 |

**Key cue:** This movement requires significant shoulder flexibility. If you feel shoulder impingement (sharp pinching), stop — it means the shoulders aren't mobile enough yet. Spend more time on the thoracic and shoulder mobility in your daily practice first. Some discomfort from the stretch is normal. Pain is not.

**Why this movement for body control:** It takes you through positions your nervous system has likely never experienced. That unfamiliarity is the point — it expands your body map.

#### Elbow Lever
*What it builds: balance, core tension, proprioception, shoulder stability. Surprisingly accessible — it's mostly a skill, not a strength movement.*

| Stage | What it looks like | Target |
|-------|--------------------|--------|
| 1 | Crouch, place hands flat, drive one elbow into hip, lean forward | Find the tipping point |
| 2 | Both elbows into hips, tuck both knees, balance | 3×5s |
| 3 | One leg extends | 3×5s |
| 4 | Both legs extend (full elbow lever) | 3×5–10s |

**Key cue:** The elbow acts as a shelf — it sits on the meaty part of your hip/oblique. The balance point is very far forward, further than feels safe. Lean into it.

**Why this is worth doing:** It teaches you to find balance in positions that look like falling. That negotiation with gravity is core to body control.

#### Muscle-Up
*What it builds: explosive pulling power, transition coordination, full-range upper body strength. Most advanced movement here — don't rush it.*

**Prerequisite check before training this:** Can you do 8 strict pull-ups with a 3-second lowering? If no, this skill is not accessible yet. Train the pull-up progression below and return to muscle-ups when the prerequisite is met.

| Stage | What it looks like | Target |
|-------|--------------------|--------|
| 1 | High pull-up: pull until bar reaches chest (not chin) | 4×3–5 |
| 2 | Transition drill: from top of pull-up, practice rolling wrists over the bar | 3×5 |
| 3 | Jumping muscle-up: use legs to assist the transition | 3×3 |
| 4 | Full muscle-up: strict, controlled | 3×3 |

**Key cue:** The muscle-up is not a pull-up + a dip. It's a single fluid movement where you pull and simultaneously begin to rotate your wrists and push. The transition is the skill — most of the training time should be spent there, not on the pull-up itself.

### Block 2 — Foundation Movements (25–30 min)

3 sets each. Never to failure. Rest 60–90 sec between sets.

#### 1. Push-Ups
*Primary: chest, triceps, anterior shoulder. Secondary: core tension, scapular control.*

| Stage | Form | Sets × Reps |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Incline (hands on bar or bench, ~45°) | 3×10 |
| 2 | Incline (lower, ~20°) | 3×10 |
| 3 | Floor push-up | 3×8–12 |
| 4 | Archer push-up (one arm bears more load) | 3×6/side |
| 5 | Pseudo planche push-up (hands at hips, leaning forward) | 3×6 |

**Tempo:** 3 sec down, 1 sec pause at bottom, press up. Never bounce off the bottom.
**Key cue:** Body is a rigid plank from head to heels. If hips sag or hike, the set is over.
**Unlock to next stage:** 3×12 with perfect form, 2 sessions in a row.

#### 2. Pull-Ups
*Primary: lats, biceps, rear shoulder. Secondary: grip, scapular control.*

| Stage | Form | Sets × Reps |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Dead hang holds | 3×30s |
| 2 | Negative pull-up (jump to top, lower 5 sec) | 3×4–5 |
| 3 | Band-assisted pull-up | 3×6–8 |
| 4 | Strict pull-up (full hang to chin over bar) | 3×5–8 |
| 5 | Weighted pull-up | 3×5 |

**Tempo:** Always control the lowering. 3–5 sec down. Full extension at the bottom — don't shorten the range.
**Key cue:** Initiate with shoulder blades pulling down and back before the arms bend. If you start with the arms, you lose lat engagement immediately.
**Unlock to next stage:** All reps controlled, no kipping, full range, 2 sessions in a row.

#### 3. Hanging Leg Raises
*Primary: hip flexors, core. Secondary: grip endurance, body awareness in a hang.*

| Stage | Form | Sets × Reps |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Hanging knee raise (tuck knees to chest) | 3×8–10 |
| 2 | Hanging knee raise with 2 sec hold at top | 3×8 |
| 3 | Partial leg raise (legs at 45°) | 3×8 |
| 4 | Full hanging leg raise (legs to horizontal) | 3×8–10 |
| 5 | Toes to bar | 3×8 |

**Key cue:** The movement starts by curling the pelvis under, not by lifting the legs. If you lift with the hip flexors alone without the pelvic tilt, you're doing a different (lesser) exercise. Think: compress your lower abs first, then the legs follow.
**No swinging.** Ever. If you're swinging, you lost control. Stop, re-stabilise, continue.

#### 4. Inverted Rows
*Primary: mid-back, rear delts, biceps. An essential counterpart to push-ups — keeps the shoulder balanced.*

| Stage | Form | Sets × Reps |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Feet on floor, body at ~45° | 3×10 |
| 2 | Feet on floor, body more horizontal | 3×10 |
| 3 | Feet elevated (on bench or box) | 3×8–10 |
| 4 | Feet elevated, pause 2 sec at top | 3×8 |
| 5 | Feet elevated, weighted vest | 3×8 |

**Key cue:** Body stays rigid throughout — same as a push-up in reverse. Squeeze shoulder blades together at the top. If your hips sag or your lower back takes over, raise the bar height.

#### 5. Dips
*Primary: triceps, lower chest, anterior shoulder. Secondary: scapular stability, core.*

⚠️ Dips place significant load on the shoulder at the bottom position. At higher bodyweight, start conservatively and respect any shoulder discomfort.

| Stage | Form | Sets × Reps |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Bench dip (hands on bench behind you, feet on floor) | 3×10 |
| 2 | Parallel bar dip, partial range (stop before shoulders drop below elbows) | 3×6–8 |
| 3 | Parallel bar dip, full range | 3×8 |
| 4 | Dip with 2 sec pause at bottom | 3×6 |
| 5 | Weighted dip | 3×5 |

**Tempo:** 3 sec down always. Never drop fast into the bottom — that's where shoulder injuries happen.
**Key cue:** Lean slightly forward (chest down) to bias chest and avoid pure tricep loading. Keep elbows tracking back, not flaring wide.

---

### Movement Pairing Logic

These 5 movements are not random. They form a complete upper-body balance:

| Push | Pull |
|------|------|
| Push-Up (horizontal push) | Inverted Row (horizontal pull) |
| Dip (vertical push) | Pull-Up (vertical pull) |
| — | Hanging Leg Raise (core + hang) |

Always train pushes and pulls in equal volume. If you ever add sets of push-ups, add sets of rows. This is what keeps the shoulder healthy over the long term.

## Body Control Progression System

This runs separately from strength. It is not about hitting reps. It is about owning positions with increasing difficulty. Work through it at your own pace — there are no timelines, only readiness criteria.

### Stage 1 — Ground and Basic Hang (Start here)

The ability to be comfortable on the floor and in a hang is more foundational than almost any gym exercise.

**Practice (during strength sessions, or standalone):**
- Floor sitting in various positions: cross-legged, legs extended, side-sitting, deep squat — 5 min daily
- Passive hang: build toward 60s continuous
- Ground-to-stand with no hands: work on this daily in your movement practice

**Unlock criteria:**
- [ ] 60s passive hang (relaxed, not death-gripping)
- [ ] Sit on floor cross-legged → stand up without using hands or knees
- [ ] Deep squat hold: 90s, heels flat, no forward lean on hands
- [ ] 10 min total floor sitting without discomfort

### Stage 2 — Active Range and Loaded Control

The ability to move *through* range with intention, not just hold positions.

**Practice:**
- Jefferson curl (light): 3×5, very slow, one vertebra at a time
- Cossack squat: 5/side, pause in the deepest point you can control
- Scapular pull-ups: 3×10 on bar (arms stay straight, move only shoulder blades)
- Hollow body hold (full): build toward 45s

**Unlock criteria:**
- [ ] Jefferson curl: reach floor with straight legs, no pain
- [ ] Cossack squat: full range both sides without falling
- [ ] 10 scapular pull-ups (clean, controlled)
- [ ] 5 strict pull-ups (full range, no kip)
- [ ] 45s hollow body hold (legs extended)

### Stage 3 — Positions Under Load

The ability to be strong *inside* difficult positions, not just next to them.

**Practice:**
- Tuck front lever hold: 3×10s (body horizontal from bar, knees tucked)
- Tucked L-sit: 3×15s (on floor or parallettes)
- Pistol squat progression: box pistol → counterbalanced → full
- Negative pull-up: 5 sec lowering, 4–5 reps

**Unlock criteria:**
- [ ] 10s tuck front lever hold
- [ ] 20s L-sit (tucked, hips fully off floor)
- [ ] 5 pistol squats each leg (to parallel, controlled)
- [ ] 8 strict pull-ups

### Stage 4 — Coordination and Expression

The ability to combine positions into fluid movement. This is where body control becomes visible.

**Practice:**
- Muscle-up progression
- Wall handstand (build toward freestanding)
- Animal flow patterns: bear crawl, crab reach, lateral ape
- Slow Turkish get-up (with light weight or bodyweight)

**Unlock criteria:**
- [ ] Turkish get-up: smooth both sides, bodyweight or light
- [ ] 20s freestanding handstand (or consistent wall handstand)
- [ ] Fluid ground-to-stand in 3+ different ways without thought

## The Weight Loss Layer

Fat loss in this plan comes from three mechanisms, in order of impact:

1. **Zone 2 cardio** — trains your body to use fat as fuel, accumulates the largest caloric deficit, zero joint cost
2. **Swim sprints** — hormonal response to high-intensity work, elevated caloric burn post-session, preserves muscle
3. **Strength training** — preserves muscle mass during a caloric deficit, which protects your metabolic rate

**The compounding loop:** As weight decreases, relative strength improves automatically. The same muscles now move less mass. This means body control progressions that feel impossible now will open up — not because you trained harder, but because the load your system has to manage got lighter. This is why fat loss and body control are not separate goals here. They accelerate each other.

**On nutrition:** This plan does not prescribe a diet. But one principle aligns perfectly with body control: eating enough protein (roughly 1.6–2g per kg of bodyweight) preserves muscle during fat loss, which keeps your strength-to-weight ratio improving even as total weight drops.

## Auto-Regulation — Reading Your Body

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Legs heavy or sore | Skip lower strength. Do Zone 2 or movement practice only. |
| Shoulders aching | Remove pull-ups. Keep rows (different angle). Add extra shoulder mobility in movement practice. |
| Generally low energy | Movement practice + Zone 2 only. Never push strength through systemic fatigue — it impairs movement learning. |
| Sharp or joint pain | Stop that movement. Note which one. If it persists 2+ sessions, remove it. |
| Feeling sharp and fluid | This is your day. Slow the tempo, try a harder variation, push the hold time. |
| Movement practice feels easy | Go slower. Find the edge of your control. Easy means you're not at the boundary. |

---

## Minimum Effective Dose (When Life Gets Busy)

Protect these in order:

1. **Daily movement practice** — even 10 min. This is what builds body control. It compounds.
2. **2× swim sprint** — your primary fitness and fat loss engine
3. **1× Zone 2** — fat loss machinery
4. **1× full-body strength** — hip thrust + pull-up + push-up + dead bug in one 40 min session

This maintains ~80% of all four goals through any week.

---

## Progress Measures (What Actually Matters)

Track these — not weight lifted:

- Can you sit on the floor and stand up without hands? Getting easier?
- Does the deep squat feel more spacious than last month?
- Are your 25m swim times holding or improving?
- Do you feel pain in fewer places day-to-day?
- Does your body feel lighter and more responsive in the water?

These are the real metrics. The numbers in the gym are a supporting tool, not the scoreboard.
