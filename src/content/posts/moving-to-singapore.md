Six months ago I moved to Singapore. Not for a short stint or a rotation — a real move, with a lease and a local phone number and the slow process of figuring out which hawker centre has the best chicken rice near my apartment.

I've been meaning to write about it, and I keep putting it off because I'm not sure what the thesis is yet. Maybe that's the point. Some experiences don't reduce to a clean lesson. They're just... experiences.

## The First Month

The first month is all logistics. Setting up a bank account, learning the MRT system, figuring out that the air conditioning in every building is set to "arctic" for reasons I still don't fully understand.

You're too busy solving practical problems to feel homesick. It's weirdly similar to incident response — there's no time for feelings when there's a queue of tasks that need doing. The feelings come later, when the task queue empties and you're sitting alone in a very quiet apartment.

## Finding a Rhythm

The thing nobody tells you about moving abroad is that the hardest part isn't the big stuff — it's the small stuff. You know where to get groceries back home. You know which coffee shop has the good pour-over. You know which route to walk when you need to think.

In a new city, all of those micro-decisions become conscious. It's cognitively exhausting in a way that's hard to explain to people who haven't done it. You're making fifty decisions a day that used to be automatic.

But slowly — over weeks, then months — the decisions become automatic again. You find your coffee shop. You find your walking route. You start recognizing the uncle who runs the drink stall downstairs. And one day you realize you've built a new set of routines without consciously deciding to.

That's when it starts to feel like home.

## What I Didn't Expect

I didn't expect to love the proximity to nature. Singapore is a city, but it's a city built in a garden. There are trails everywhere — MacRitchie, Bukit Timah, the Southern Ridges — and they're accessible from the MRT. I've started taking my camera on weekend walks, and it's become one of my favorite things about living here.

I also didn't expect how much I'd learn from working across time zones. When your team spans multiple continents, you develop a discipline around documentation and asynchronous communication that you never needed when everyone was in the same office. It makes you a better communicator, even if the reason you got better was mostly "necessity."

## No Clean Lesson

I said at the start that I don't have a thesis. I still don't. Moving is hard and good and lonely and exciting, often on the same day. I'm glad I did it. I think.

Ask me again in a year.
