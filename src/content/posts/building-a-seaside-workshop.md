There's something about working near the water that changes how you think. The rhythm of it — waves arriving and retreating — mirrors the best kind of creative work. You push forward, then you let the idea settle. You push again.

Last month I started building a small workshop on the coast. Not a grand project — just a shed, really — but one where every decision was intentional.

## Finding the Right Materials

The challenge with coastal building is salt air. It corrodes metal, warps untreated wood, and fogs windows. Every material choice is a bet against the elements. You learn quickly that **the cheapest option is never cheap** when the sea gets involved.

I settled on western red cedar for the frame and cladding — naturally resistant to rot, beautiful grain, and it weathers to a silver-grey that looks like it belongs on the coastline. The hardware is all marine-grade stainless. Expensive, but it won't be a rust stain in two years.

## The View from Inside

The whole point of the workshop is the window. One large pane facing west, over the water. Everything else — the workbench, the shelving, the power tools — is arranged to let that view breathe.

There's a lesson in this for everything I build: **start with the view**. What's the thing you actually want to see or experience? Design everything else around that. The infrastructure serves the view, not the other way around.
