I've been an engineer long enough to know that the best systems aren't the cleverest ones — they're the ones that are simple enough to run reliably. Turns out, the same principle applies to fitness.

## The Problem with Motivation

Most fitness plans assume motivation. "Just push through." "Stay disciplined." "Trust the process." This is the equivalent of telling an on-call engineer to "just be more careful." It doesn't scale and it doesn't survive stress.

What does scale is **systems.** Defaults. Constraints. Automated decisions.

## The Low-Decision Approach

I redesigned my nutrition around a single principle: **reduce decisions, not calories.** Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **Protein first, every meal.** Not "try to eat enough protein." A hard rule. Palm-and-a-half of meat or fish is ~40-45g. Three meals at that level plus a snack and I'm at 150-170g without thinking about it.
- **2-3 default meals on rotation.** I don't decide what to eat — I pick from a list I've already validated. Eggs and Greek yogurt for breakfast. Chicken and rice for lunch. Lean protein and vegetables for dinner. Boring? Yes. Sustainable? Absolutely.
- **Track for a month, then eyeball.** Counting calories forever is not a system — it's a chore. But counting for a few weeks teaches you what portions look like. After that, you can estimate accurately enough.

## Training Around Constraints

I have a shoulder injury that rules out overhead pressing and heavy loads. Instead of seeing this as a limitation, I treated it as a **design constraint** — the same way you'd design software around an API limitation.

The result: a program built entirely around posterior-chain work (glutes, hamstrings, back) and core stability. Dumbbells only. Low-impact cardio (cycling, swimming). A daily mobility habit that takes 5 minutes.

It's not optimal in some theoretical sense. But it's **executable** — I can do every session without pain, which means I actually do them.

## The Meta-Lesson

The people who get fit aren't the ones with the best plans. They're the ones who follow a good-enough plan consistently. This is the same insight that makes reliable infrastructure: **boring, consistent execution beats heroic effort every time.**

Build a system you'll actually follow. Remove decisions. Embrace constraints. Trust the boring process.
